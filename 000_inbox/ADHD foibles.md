**2. Embrace "good enough" processing over perfect systems**
ADHD brains often abandon systems that require consistent perfection. Instead of processing everything to completion, aim for moving items just one step forward. A note that gets a single tag or one connecting thought is better than 50 notes waiting for "proper" processing.

**3. Work with momentum and interest, not against resistance**
Traditional GTD assumes you can process items neutrally and systematically. ADHD brains need emotional engagement. Process what pulls you in that moment - the system should flex around your attention, not vice versa.

## Specific Strategies

**Strategy 1: The "One Question Game"**
Instead of processing items through a flowchart, ask just ONE question per item: "What's the smallest interesting thing I could do with this right now?" Not useful, not important - *interesting*. This could be adding a funny tag, connecting it to something random, or writing one reaction sentence.

*Why it works:* ADHD brains engage with novelty and play. By making it a game focused on interest rather than utility, you bypass the resistance to "productivity tasks." The low bar ("smallest thing") prevents overwhelm while still creating forward movement.

**Strategy 2: Voice Processing Sessions**
Set a timer for 5-10 minutes and process by talking out loud to yourself (or recording). Read each item and just say whatever comes to mind - reactions, connections, actions, or even "I have no idea what to do with this and that's fine."

*Why it works:* Speaking engages different neural pathways than reading/writing and often bypasses the executive function bottleneck. It's harder for your brain to "freeze" when you're in verbal flow. Plus, ADHD brains often process better through externalization.

**Strategy 3: The "Random Three" Method**
Use a random number generator to pick exactly three items to process. Process only those three, in any way that feels right in the moment. Could be deleting, quick actions, or just adding context. Then you're done for the day.

*Why it works:* Removes decision fatigue about what to process and creates a clear, achievable endpoint. The randomness adds novelty (ADHD catnip) and prevents your brain from pre-emptively resisting specific items. Three is enough to feel productive but not trigger overwhelm.

**Strategy 4: Context Injection Processing**
Instead of processing items in isolation, always have something else open - a YouTube video, podcast, or music. When you hit resistance on an item, let your attention briefly shift to the background content, then return.

*Why it works:* ADHD brains often function better with controlled distraction - it occupies the part of your brain that would otherwise generate resistance. The ability to briefly escape reduces the feeling of being trapped in a boring task.

**Strategy 5: The "Future Self Message" Approach**
When you can't process something properly, just write a quick message to your future self about it: "Future me: this is about that API problem. You were frustrated but had an idea about caching. No action needed, just wanted you to remember the vibe."

*Why it works:* Removes the pressure to make decisions or create perfect taxonomies. It's just communication, which feels more natural than "processing." These messages often contain more useful context than formal processing would capture, and they're actually enjoyable to rediscover later.
