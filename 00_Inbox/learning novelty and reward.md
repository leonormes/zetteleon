## Learning, Novelty and Reward

This is a fascinating question that touches on some deep aspects of human psychology and neuroscience. Let me help explain what’s happening in your brain and suggest ways to maintain engagement with learned concepts.

The phenomenon you’re describing is deeply rooted in how our brains process novelty and reward. When we encounter something mysterious or unknown, our brain’s reward system releases dopamine \- not just in anticipation of understanding, but specifically because of the uncertainty. This is particularly pronounced in individuals with ADHD, where the dopamine system works somewhat differently from neurotypical brains.

Think of it like this: Your brain is like an explorer who gets excited by seeing a mysterious island on the horizon. The mystery itself creates a state of heightened engagement \- “What could be there? What amazing things might I discover?” This anticipation creates a dopamine response that feels especially rewarding to the ADHD brain, which is often seeking such stimulation.

However, once you reach the island and map it out completely (understand the concept), that sense of mystery evaporates. The dopamine response diminishes because there’s no more uncertainty to engage with. This is why concepts can feel less interesting once you understand them \- the brain’s reward system is no longer being activated by the unknown.

But here’s where we can shift our perspective: Understanding how something works doesn’t make it less magnificent \- it just changes how we appreciate it. Let’s take your example of Linux kernel namespacing. When you first encountered it, it might have seemed like magic \- “How can containers be isolated yet share the same kernel?” The mystery was intriguing. Now that you understand it’s “just” a way of partitioning kernel resources, it might seem mundane.

However, consider this alternative viewpoint: The “mundane” solution is actually more impressive than the imagined magic. Think about it \- humans figured out how to take something as complex as a kernel and systematically partition it in a way that creates isolated environments while maintaining efficiency. The elegance of this solution, how it builds on fundamental principles to solve complex problems, is actually more remarkable than any magical explanation could be.

To maintain engagement with learned concepts, try these approaches:

1. Become a knowledge archaeologist: Instead of just learning how something works, dig into its history. Why was this solution developed? What problems did people face before? What other approaches were tried?
2. Look for the deeper patterns: Once you understand a concept, use it as a lens to understand other systems. How does the principle of namespacing apply to other areas of computing or even other fields entirely?
3. Teach and explore: Use your knowledge to help others understand. You’ll often find that explaining concepts leads to new insights and questions you hadn’t considered.
4. Connect the dots: Challenge yourself to find novel applications or connections between different concepts you’ve learned. This creates new mysteries to explore within familiar territory.
5. Appreciate the elegance: Train yourself to find beauty in simplicity. When something turns out to be “simple,” that often means it’s an elegant solution to a complex problem.

Remember, the initial mystery is just one type of intellectual pleasure. There’s also the joy of mastery, the satisfaction of seeing patterns across different domains, and the excitement of building new things with your knowledge. Instead of seeking only the dopamine hit of novelty, you can cultivate appreciation for these other forms of engagement.

Think of it like learning music: At first, you might be mystified by how musicians create beautiful sounds. Once you learn that it’s “just” patterns of notes following certain rules, you could become disappointed \- or you could appreciate how these simple elements combine to create infinite possibilities for expression.
