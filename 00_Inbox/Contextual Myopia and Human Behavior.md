## A More Formal Description: Contextual Myopia and Goal Displacement

What you are describing is a powerful form of **cognitive myopia** (short-sightedness) that is highly dependent on one's immediate environment. It's the human tendency to over-emphasise present concerns and local information at the expense of distant, more abstract, but often more important, objectives.

Here are the key mechanisms at play, which might help you articulate your model:

### 1. The Focusing Illusion

This is a cognitive bias where people place too much importance on one aspect of an event, causing them to misjudge the reality of the situation. Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman summarised it as: "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is, while you are thinking about it."

- **In your argument example:** The two people are focusing intensely on the perceived slight or the "principle" of the matter. In that moment, their brain allocates it an importance level of 10/10, completely ignoring the fact that an hour later, it will be a 1/10, if remembered at all.
- **In your dev team example:** The team is focusing on a complex technical problem or the elegance of their code. The intellectual challenge is so engaging that it becomes the most important thing, eclipsing the actual goal: solving a user's problem.

### 2. Goal Displacement

This is a concept from organisational sociology. It describes what happens when the original goals of an organisation (the *ends*) are replaced by the rules, procedures, and processes that were meant to achieve them (the *means*).

- Your development team is a classic example. The goal is "create value for users". The means is "write code, complete tickets, hold stand-ups". **Goal displacement** occurs when the team derives its sense of achievement from completing the means (closing a ticket) rather than achieving the end (confirming users are happy). They feel successful even if the product is a failure.

### 3. The Law of Triviality (or "Bikeshedding")

Coined by C. Northcote Parkinson, this principle states that members of an organisation give disproportionate weight to trivial issues. He famously illustrated this with a committee tasked with approving plans for a nuclear power plant. They spend minutes on the multi-million-pound reactor design (as it's too complex for most to grasp) but argue for an hour about the material for the staff bike shed (as everyone has an opinion on it).

- This relates to your escalating argument. The initial trigger was likely trivial, something both parties felt qualified to have a strong opinion on. The "made up important things" are a form of bikeshedding—focusing on manageable, albeit irrelevant, details of the conflict.

### 4. Commitment and Consistency & The Sunk Cost Fallacy

Once people commit to a position or a course of action, even in a small way, they feel internal and external pressure to behave consistently with that commitment. The more effort, time, or emotion they invest (sunk costs), the more they will rationalise its importance to justify the investment.

- In the argument, neither person wants to be the first to admit their escalating feelings are disproportionate to the initial event. They have invested their ego and must now see it through, manufacturing reasons why it is a vital matter of principle.

## Where Camus Goes Next: The Response to the Absurd

While you have masterfully described the *condition* of being alive, Camus was most concerned with how one should *live* once this realisation dawns. He saw three possible responses to the Absurd:

- **Passion & Freedom:** To realise that in a universe with no ultimate rules or purpose, you are completely free. How have philosophers countered Camus?

Philosophers have countered Albert Camus from several angles, often accepting his initial diagnosis of the Absurd but strongly disagreeing with his conclusions about how to live with it. The critiques can be broadly categorised into three main areas: philosophical, political, and theological.

## 1. Philosophical Critiques (Primarily from Existentialists)

The most famous philosophical counter comes from his contemporary and friend-turned-rival, **Jean-Paul Sartre**. While both are often grouped as existentialists (a label Camus himself rejected), their disagreement was profound.

- **Creating Meaning vs. Living with Meaninglessness:** Sartre's core tenet was "existence precedes essence." He argued that since life has no inherent meaning, we are "condemned to be free" to *create* our own meaning and values through our choices and actions. For Sartre, creating a personal essence is the fundamental project of life. Camus, in contrast, argued that any attempt to create ultimate meaning is a futile and evasive "philosophical suicide." For him, the goal is not to solve or escape the Absurd by creating meaning, but to live in constant, lucid revolt against it, keeping the conflict between our desire and the world's silence alive.
- **Human Nature:** Sartre believed there is no fixed human nature; we define it through our existence.Camus, particularly in *The Rebel*, argues for a shared, pre-existing human solidarity. The act of a single person rebelling against an injustice, he says, is done on behalf of all humanity. This implies a common value that Sartre's philosophy, in its purest form, would reject.

## 2. Political Critiques (The Rebel vs. The Revolutionary)

This is where the friendship with Sartre definitively shattered. Camus's 1951 book, *The Rebel*, was a direct attack on what he saw as the logical endpoint of revolutionary ideology.

- **Rejection of Revolutionary Violence:** Camus condemned revolutionary movements, particularly Marxism-Leninism, for sacrificing individual lives in the present for a hypothetical, perfect future. He argued that when a revolution deifies a historical endpoint (like a classless society), it justifies absolute violence and inevitably leads to tyranny and the police state. This was seen as a direct attack on the intellectual Left in France, which was broadly sympathetic to the communist cause.
- **Sartre's Marxist Counter:** Sartre, along with his followers, savaged *The Rebel*.He accused Camus of being a naive moralist, a "bourgeois" intellectual who was afraid to get his hands dirty. For Sartre and other Marxists, violence could be a necessary tool to overthrow an oppressive system. They saw Camus's position as a betrayal, effectively siding with the oppressive status quo by condemning the only means of its destruction. Camus's focus on individual rebellion and moral limits was seen as politically ineffective and cowardly.

## 3. Theological Critiques (The Leap of Faith)

Camus famously dismissed religion as a form of "philosophical suicide"—a "leap of faith" taken to escape the anxiety of the Absurd. Religious philosophers, particularly Christian existentialists, countered this directly.

- **The Cause of the Absurd:** From a Christian perspective, the "absurd" state Camus describes—the gulf between our longing for meaning and the world's indifference—is not the natural state of things but a consequence of human sin and the Fall. The disjunction is real, but it has a cause and, more importantly, a solution.
- **Incarnation over Revolt:** Christian thinkers argue that Camus misunderstands the "leap." It is not an escape from reality, but an embrace of a deeper reality revealed through faith. They posit that the answer to the silent universe is not a human-generated revolt, but the *Incarnation* —the idea that God entered into human suffering in the person of Jesus. In this view, God is not an absent bystander but a participant who resolves the Absurd not by eliminating it, but by sharing in it and ultimately overcoming it. They argue Camus's noble revolt is a lonely, insufficient response to a problem that has already been answered.

## 4. Critiques of Inconsistency and Vagueness

Other philosophers have pointed to logical weaknesses in Camus's own framework.

- **The Basis for Revolt:** If the world is truly without inherent value, on what basis does Camus's rebel say "no" to oppression? The very act of rebellion and the call for moderation and solidarity seem to presuppose certain values (like justice, dignity, freedom) that the Absurd world itself does not provide. Critics argue that Camus smuggles these values back into his system without adequately justifying them, thereby making his own "leap of faith" into humanism.
- **Feminist Critiques:** More contemporary critiques have focused on the portrayal of women in his work. In novels like *The Stranger*, female characters are often depicted as passive, physical objects for the male protagonist's experience or observation. They lack the agency and depth of the male "Absurd heroes," leading critics to argue that Camus's vision of humanity and rebellion was primarily male-centric.

In essence, while many thinkers respect Camus's powerful diagnosis of the modern condition, they counter that his prescribed response is either politically naive (the Marxists), theologically incomplete (the Christians), or philosophically inconsistent (the Existentialists and others).

## The Critique of the Uncaused Cause

**Your point:** "Ok, there is a god, what caused god? What drives god?"

You are applying the principle of causality to God himself. The classical theological response to this is the concept of God as a **Necessary Being** or an **Uncaused Cause**.

- **The Theological Argument:** Developed by philosophers like Aristotle (as the "Unmoved Mover") and Thomas Aquinas, this argument states that the chain of causation cannot go back infinitely. It must terminate in a being that is not *contingent* (i.e., it does not depend on anything else for its existence) but is *necessary*. This being's very nature or essence *is* to exist. It cannot *not* exist. Therefore, the question "What caused God?" is a category error, like asking "What's north of the North Pole?". God is presented as the ultimate brute fact, the terminating point of all "why" questions.
- **Your Counter-Argument:** As you've implicitly argued, for a sceptic, this feels like an instance of "special pleading." The theist insists everything must have a cause, and then presents one thing—God—that is conveniently exempt from this rule.
