Source: [[The Two Views of Maths]]

The Idea (In Detail):

- The Mechanic ([[Applicative maths is the use of pre-discovered patterns as tools|Applicative Maths]]): A master of existing systems. They know the car inside and out and can expertly apply tools to diagnose and fix known problems.
- The Engineer ([[Generative maths is the creative discovery of abstract patterns|Generative Maths]]): A master of first principles. They understand thermodynamics and materials science to design a completely new type of engine from scratch.

My Thoughts & Implications:

This analogy is powerful because it respects both roles. The mechanic is essential and highly skilled, as is the engineer. It clarifies that the two views of maths are not "basic" vs "advanced", but rather two fundamentally different jobs or modes of thinking. The [[Public perception of maths is incomplete|public interacts with the "mechanics" of maths]], so they naturally assume that's all there is. This analogy could be a useful tool for explaining the disconnect to others.

Like other effective analogies (such as [[The Target Analogy for Accuracy and Precision]]), this comparison helps make abstract concepts concrete and memorable. However, as with all [[Flawed Mental Models Limit Mastery|mental models]], we must be careful not to push the analogy too far - it's a useful simplification, not a perfect representation of mathematical practice.
