### How to Manage Your Interpretation

Your "failing" is not that you think this; it's that you are treating your *interpretation* as a *fact*. The fact is: you said "no," and she got annoyed. Your interpretation is: "She's immature and thinks I have no say."

When she reacts, her annoyance might not be directed *at your right to say no*. It might simply be a moment of pure, unanalysed **frustration** or **disappointment**.

- She wanted a thing (the light on, you to tidy).
- She didn't get the thing.
- The immediate, human reaction to not getting what you want is frustration.

Her reaction is not "How dare *he* tell me what to do." Her reaction is "Damn, now I can't [see my book / have a tidy room]."

#### 2. Separate Her Feeling from Your Boundary

This is the most critical part. You are mistaking her emotional reaction as a *challenge* to your boundary.

> **Two things can be true at the same time:**
>
> 1. You can have a completely valid and reasonable boundary (e.g., "No, I'm not turning the TV off," or "No, I'm not tidying that right now").
> 2. She can have a completely valid emotional reaction of being annoyed or disappointed by that boundary.

Her disappointment does not make your "no" wrong. Her being "affronted" does not erase your "say." You *had* your say. You said "no." Your boundary *worked*.

You are allowing her *reaction* to your "no" to make you feel as if your "no" wasn't (or shouldn't have been) allowed. Stop giving her reaction that power.
