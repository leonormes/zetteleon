Neuroscientific investigations into programming often focus on tasks such as code comprehension, debugging, and logical reasoning. Early findings suggest that programming is not localized to a single brain area but rather involves a network of regions, many of which are associated with domain-general cognitive functions. For instance, studies have highlighted the engagement of the Multiple Demand (MD) system—a network of frontal and parietal brain regions typically active during tasks requiring executive functions like problem-solving, working memory, and attention—during code comprehension. Other research points to the involvement of brain areas related to mathematical reasoning and language processing, although the latter's role appears distinct from natural language understanding. A particularly interesting finding is the role of the insula, a region involved in error monitoring and awareness, in detecting bugs in code.
