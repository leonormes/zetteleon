You've hit on the central point of Dietrich Bonhoeffer's argument. He is indeed using the word "stupid" in a very specific philosophical sense, which is quite different from our everyday use of it to mean being unintelligent or "thick."

Based on his writings, here's a breakdown of what he means:

## Bonhoeffer's Definition of Stupidity

For Bonhoeffer, **stupidity is not an intellectual defect but a moral one**. It's a form of **wilful thoughtlessness** or **uncritical conformity**.

He defines it as a state where a person, often under the influence of a powerful group or ideology, gives up their own critical thinking and inner independence. They stop judging situations for themselves and instead rely on slogans, catchphrases, and pre-packaged opinions. It is not about a lack of innate intelligence; in fact, Bonhoeffer noted that intellectually brilliant people could be profoundly stupid in this moral sense.

The key characteristics are:

- **It is acquired, not innate.** It's a choice people make, or allow to be made for them.
- **It is a group phenomenon.** It thrives in mobs, parties, and movements where individual responsibility is handed over to a leader or an ideology.
- **It is dangerous.** Because the "stupid" person is not thinking for themselves, they are self-satisfied and easily provoked into aggression to defend the slogans they have adopted. They are impervious to reason.

---

## Is There a Better Word

Yes, several English phrases probably get closer to Bonhoeffer's intended meaning than the single word "stupid." Good alternatives would be:

- **Wilful Blindness:** This captures the sense that the person is choosing not to see the truth.
- **Thoughtlessness:** As in, a complete lack of independent thought.
- **Moral Idiocy:** This highlights the ethical dimension of the failure to think.
- **Intellectual Obstinacy:** This describes the stubborn refusal to engage with facts or reason that contradict their adopted worldview.

---

## Why Use the Word 'Stupid'

There are a couple of likely reasons why Bonhoeffer (and his translators) stuck with such a loaded word.

1. **Provocation:** The word is deliberately shocking. By using a common insult like "stupid" for a complex moral failing, Bonhoeffer forces the reader to stop and question their own assumptions. It's a powerful rhetorical device.
2. **Emotional Truth:** He was writing this from a Nazi prison, observing how his nation had fallen into tyranny. "Stupidity" ("Dummheit" in German) captures the profound frustration and exasperation of watching people—many of them otherwise clever—uncritically follow a destructive path. It's a word that conveys not just a philosophical concept, but a visceral, emotional reality.

---

## Is It a Poor Translation

No, it is not a poor translation. The German word Bonhoeffer used is **Dummheit**, which translates directly to "stupidity" or "dumbness." The problem isn't one of inaccurate translation, but that the philosophical context is lost without further explanation. The translators have correctly chosen the most direct equivalent, leaving it to the reader to understand the deeper meaning Bonhoeffer intended.

## Doesn't Dogma Fall Under His Definition of "Stupidity"

This is an excellent and crucial question that gets to the heart of Bonhoeffer's thought. You're right to see an apparent contradiction. On the surface, blindly following any dogma seems to fit his definition of "stupidity" perfectly.

However, Bonhoeffer made a critical distinction between **institutional religion** and **authentic faith**.

- **"Stupid" Religion:** For Bonhoeffer, religion becomes a form of "stupidity" when it involves **unthinking conformity**. This happens when a person or an institution uses dogma to escape personal responsibility, to justify inaction, or to blindly follow a powerful authority. He saw this happening all around him in the German church, which largely capitulated to the Nazis. This, to him, was the ultimate example of religious "stupidity"—surrendering one's moral conscience to the power of the group.
- **Authentic Faith:** In contrast, Bonhoeffer championed what he called "costly grace" and a "religionless Christianity." He argued that true Christian faith wasn't about blindly adhering to a set of rules passed down by an institution. Instead, it was a living, personal commitment that forced an individual to think critically and act ethically in the real world, even at great personal cost.

For Bonhoeffer, his faith didn't give him slogans to repeat; it gave him the moral imperative to act responsibly. His decision to participate in the plot to assassinate Hitler was a radical act of personal conscience, born from his faith but in defiance of simplistic religious rules like "thou shalt not kill." He was wrestling with his beliefs in a complex world, not thoughtlessly applying them.

So, in essence, Bonhoeffer would argue that the enemy isn't belief or dogma itself, but the **surrender of one's critical and moral faculties** to any power—be it a political party or a religious institution. Authentic faith, in his view, was the very antidote to this kind of "stupidity."
